Principal Investigator

Brett Kroncke, Ph.D.

Assistant Professor of Medicine · Director, Kroncke Lab

Vanderbilt University Medical Center · Vanderbilt Center for Arrhythmia Research and Therapeutics (VanCART) · Department of Pharmacology · Center for Structural Biology · Vanderbilt Genomics Institute · Data Science Institute

brett.kroncke@gmail.com

Brett founded the Kroncke Lab in 2019 to build a translational genomics program focused on molecular cardiogenetics, leading a multidisciplinary team dedicated to understanding how genetic variation shapes individual risk for arrhythmia and adverse clinical outcomes.

Research focus. We develop high-throughput experimental pipelines, CRISPR-edited induced pluripotent stem cell models, and predictive statistical frameworks to resolve variants of uncertain significance in ion channel genes such as KCNH2 and SCN5A. Our group leverages polygenic background to interpret penetrance in stem cell-derived cardiomyocytes and has advanced statistical methods that translate functional and genomic data into individualized risk profiles. Most recently, the lab has built an open-source, LLM-assisted pipeline for literature curation and variant interpretation, and is extending these methods from long QT and Brugada syndromes to atrial fibrillation.

Innovation and translation. Brett is the inventor on the 2022 patent Bayesian Method to Estimate Variant-Induced Disease Penetrance (US-20220406461-A1) and developed VariantBrowser.org, a clinical and research portal that disseminates penetrance estimates and functional insights to clinicians, genetic counselors, and researchers.

Grants and awards. Current and recent funding includes NIH R01HL160863 (2022–2027), NIH K99/R00 HL135442 (2017–2022), and an American Heart Association Career Development Award (2021–2024).

Education and training. Brett earned his Ph.D. in Biophysics from the University of Virginia, where he developed methods to measure membrane protein flexibility and structure, and completed postdoctoral training at Vanderbilt that integrated structural biology with cardiac arrhythmia genetics and predictive modeling.

Research Assistants

DM

Devyn Mitchell

Research Assistant

devyn.w.mitchell@vumc.org

Since joining the lab in June 2019, Devyn has become a key contributor to our research on the genetic underpinnings of heart arrhythmias, focusing on the high-throughput experimental characterization of KCNH2 variants through heterologous expression in HEK cells and endogenous studies in SH-SY5Y cells. His initiative enabled the lab to pivot to induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) as a research model — reprogramming PBMCs to hiPSCs and leading the characterization of differentiated cardiomyocytes, including functional analysis on the Nanion CardioExcyte96.

Former Lab Members

Rizwan Ullah, Ph.D.

Postdoctoral Fellow · joined 2019

Dr. Ullah earned his Ph.D. in Zoology from Aligarh Muslim University in 2019, where he characterized cathepsin L, a cysteine protease from the liver fluke Fasciola gigantica, identified candidate therapeutic molecules for parasite control, and used the protein as a diagnostic antigen to detect field infections.

LV

Loren Vanags

Research Assistant · joined 2018

Loren was instrumental in establishing the lab's wet-lab facilities and advancing our research on the genetic determinants of heart arrhythmias. Her expertise in CRISPR-Cas9 editing of induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) was pivotal in characterizing arrhythmia-associated variants. She developed and validated functional assays for the RYR2 gene in both HEK293 cells and human iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes (hiPSC-CMs), ran the lab's fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) experiments, and helped generate the most comprehensive map of variant effects for the cardiac potassium channel gene KCNH2.

Krystian Kozek, Ph.D.

Kroncke Lab · 2018–2019

Krystian completed his Ph.D. in the lab of David Weaver in the Department of Pharmacology, Vanderbilt University, in 2018, focusing on modulation of GIRK channels by small molecules. In the Kroncke Lab he refined methods for high-throughput characterization of missense variants in the cardiac potassium channel Kv11.1 (gene KCNH2/hERG).
